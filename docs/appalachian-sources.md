# Appalachian Frolic source and provenance notes

## Scope and cultural posture

Appalachian Frolic presents three **gameplay profiles**, not an authoritative
taxonomy. “Flatfoot,” “buck,” and “clogging” overlap, shift by region and
teacher, and can describe different practices in different communities. The
mode’s labels are deliberately qualified in the UI and data.

The cultural-history foundation is creativity **and** continuity: Appalachian
dance is a living family of social practices shaped by ongoing exchange, not a
sealed survival from one supposedly pure source. The project brief’s Spalding
and Jamison reviews informed that posture. Public review records used during
implementation:

- [Susan Eike Spalding, *Appalachian Dance: Creativity and Continuity in Six Communities* — review record](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/311164057_Susan_Eike_Spalding_Appalachian_Dance_Creativity_and_Continuity_in_Six_Communities)
- [Phil Jamison, *Hoedowns, Reels, and Frolics* — review in the Journal of Folklore Research](https://scholarworks.iu.edu/journals/index.php/jfrr/article/view/38388)

Those reviews support a history involving European, African and African
American, Afro-Caribbean, and Native American exchange. They do not support a
single origin story, a racial-purity claim, or treating every Appalachian
community as interchangeable.

The two uploaded review PDFs named in the project brief were not copied into
the repository and are not redistributed here. This file links the public
review records and practitioner sources instead.

## Practitioner and primary-reference set

- [Phil Jamison’s dancer archive](https://www.philjamison.com/dancer-videos) —
  individual dancer histories, overlapping vocabulary, regional backsteps,
  Robert Dotson’s Walking Step, John Reeves’s documented repertoire, and
  Finney’s personal signature “The Chicken.”
- [NEA Heritage Fellow Thomas Maupin](https://www.arts.gov/honors/heritage/thomas-maupin) —
  bent knees, toe/heel articulation, scissor paths, restraint, and feet acting
  as part of the band.
- [NEA Heritage Fellow Robert Spicer](https://www.arts.gov/honors/heritage/robert-spicer) —
  low, economical, improvised dancing and relaxed arms used for balance.
- [NEA comparison of Appalachian percussive styles](https://www.arts.gov/stories/blog/2017/neaheritage-spotlight-appalachian-percussive-dancing) —
  close-to-floor personal flatfoot/buck practice, more projected staged
  clogging examples, and explicit variation in terminology.
- [Folkstreams, *The Floating Dancer*](https://www.folkstreams.net/films/floating-dancer)
  and its [transcript](https://www.folkstreams.net/contexts/floating-dancer-transcript) —
  close-to-floor travel, Walking Step, toe-drop detail, and individual style.
- [PBS, Appalachian flatfooting](https://www.pbs.org/video/life-in-virginias-appalachia-flatfooting-delhdg/) —
  social setting and low percussive movement reference.
- [Double Toe beginning lessons](https://www.doubletoe.com/Learn.htm) —
  present-day instructional vocabulary including double toe, basic, rock step,
  and rocking chair.
- [Clogging Resource beginning steps](https://www.cloggingresource.com/steps/beginning-steps) —
  present-day beginning-step terminology and pattern structures.

Footage is movement reference only. No third-party frames, recordings, or audio
are shipped.

## Movement-family provenance

| Game family | Movement principle used | Sources and boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Walking Step | Alternating, musically useful foundation with clear weight transfer | Jamison’s Robert Dotson archive entry and *The Floating Dancer*. This is a general game foundation, not a reconstruction of Dotson’s performance. |
| Forward-and-Back Sliding Walk | Low, smooth travel with both feet close to the board | NEA style comparison, Spicer, and *The Floating Dancer*. |
| Shuffle | Brush out/return with a supported body | Jamison archive plus Double Toe and Clogging Resource instructional vocabulary. |
| Double Shuffle | Two controlled brush-return cycles with density restraint | The name occurs in Jamison’s John Reeves entry; mechanics use general brush principles and do not claim Reeves’s personal version. |
| Backstep | Rearward rhythmic weight exchange | Regional backsteps appear across the archive. The game intentionally uses no regional or personal surname. |
| Chug | Compact rebound and weight drive, not a vertical cartoon hop | Double Toe and Clogging Resource instruction, checked against low-body practitioner footage. |
| Heel-Toe Change | Audible heel/toe distinction under a supported pelvis | Maupin’s toe/heel emphasis, NEA comparison, and current instruction sources. |
| Drag-Slide | Deliberate non-planted travel followed by a fixed plant | Flatfoot footage, Spicer, and *The Floating Dancer*. |
| Rock Step / Ball Change | Two-foot weight exchange with a breathable cadence | Double Toe and Clogging Resource instruction. |
| Double Step | Clear repeated brush-step pattern | Double Toe and Clogging Resource instruction. |
| Triple Step | Three-part pattern with an authored recovery | Clogging Resource instruction; presented as one game pattern, not a universal definition. |
| Crisscross / Scissor | Readable crossing paths with distinct feet and a supporting leg | Maupin’s documented scissor-step principle and archive footage. It is not assigned a personal historical name. |
| Turnaround | Phrase-ending cross, transfer, and musical resolution | Original Kaki-Dance gameplay choreography informed by eight-bar strain endings. |
| Controlled Ending | Anticipation, final contact, and visible weight settle | Original Kaki-Dance gameplay choreography. |

## Explicit exclusions

- “The Chicken” remains identified as one dancer’s personal signature and is
  not a generic move.
- Broken Wing, Wring the Chicken’s Neck, Dust the Pigeon Toe, and regional
  named backsteps are not mechanically invented from their names.
- Potentially derogatory historical labels do not appear as casual move names.
- The stage contains no moonshine gag, missing-teeth joke, fake dialect,
  Western-saloon treatment, or cultural-purity claim.

## Review still required

This work still needs review by Appalachian percussive dancers from more than
one community. Priority review questions are:

1. Do the three qualified profiles remain useful without flattening regional
   and individual variation?
2. Does Flatfoot read as restraint and texture rather than merely “slow Clog”?
3. Do Buck’s ball-of-foot and scissor mechanics feel credible?
4. Does the staged-leaning Clog profile avoid implying that all clogging is
   loud, standardized, competitive, or team-based?
5. Are the movement names, contextual notes, stage, and band interactions
   welcoming and free of unintended stereotypes?
